\begin{abstract}
Tactile residuals are usually treated as scalar alarms: if measured touch differs from predicted touch, the robot slows, replans, or updates a model. This collapses two physically different causes. A residual caused by internal dynamics mismatch should trigger adaptation; a residual caused by unexpected external contact should trigger recovery or interruption. We propose calibrated causal tactile residuals (\method{}), a controller-facing diagnostic interface that interprets residual energy together with residual shape, onset timing, spatial jump, persistence, and contact-graph inconsistency. The method does not claim universal tactile causality. It asks a narrower question: when a contact-structured predictor already exposes an active contact graph, can calibrated residual features choose between model adaptation and external-contact recovery better than alarm baselines? We answer with a full-scale deterministic benchmark crossing 16 contact-source families, 6 tactile sensor models, 7 calibration regimes, 5 manipulation phases, 5 material regimes, 7 policies, and 3 noise regimes. The benchmark contains \rowN{} compact condition rows representing \trajN{} trajectory-level evaluations and \frameN{} frame-level residual decisions. Across the full suite, \method{} obtains 0.738 external-contact F1 and 0.621 decision utility, compared with 0.597 and 0.491 for raw residual energy and 0.550 and 0.451 for an uncertainty gate. The oracle source classifier reaches 0.949 F1, leaving a visible but quantified gap. The largest positive finding is not that tactile residuals solve contact diagnosis; it is that calibrated residual geometry and contact-graph inconsistency turn touch residuals into a useful response-selection signal while exposing the exact calibration regimes where the signal remains fragile.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Contact-rich manipulation is full of tactile surprises. A gripper sliding along a fixture, a soft object deforming under load, a person brushing the hand during insertion, and an actuator ripple in a stiff grasp can all produce residuals between predicted and measured tactile observations. A controller that treats every residual as an alarm must choose one generic reaction. That reaction is often wrong. If the residual was produced by internal model error, the robot should adapt its model, update a compliance estimate, or continue under a safer impedance. If the residual was produced by an external contact, the robot should stop, recover, or hand control to a different safety mode.

This paper studies tactile residuals as causal diagnostic evidence rather than scalar surprise. The word causal is used in a deliberately narrow engineering sense: the residual is interpreted with respect to the contact structure that generated it, and the output is a response cause, not an abstract latent variable. The proposed interface distinguishes two response classes. The internal class contains mismatch between the robot's predictor and the object or actuation state. The external class contains contact from the environment, a person, a neighboring object, or a constraint not present in the current contact graph. The goal is not to prove causality from raw tactile pixels. The goal is to create a calibrated residual interface that can decide whether a controller should adapt or recover.

The central difficulty is calibration. Residual energy alone is attractive because it is easy to compute and often monotone with surprise. But energy is not source-specific. A heavy object with underestimated inertia and a side collision can have similar energy. Residual shape and timing are more diagnostic: an external contact tends to introduce asymmetric load, a spatial jump, contact order violation, or a sudden onset inconsistent with the active graph. Those features are also more brittle. Optical gels, magnetic skins, capacitive arrays, soft barometric fingertips, and wrist force-torque proxies report shape and timing with different biases. Feature attenuation, spatial blur, cross-axis coupling, and quantization can erase the distinction that made the residual useful.

We therefore frame \method{} as a calibrated interface rather than a universal classifier. Given predicted touch $\hat{y}_t$, measured touch $y_t$, and the active contact graph $g_t$, the residual is summarized by a feature vector
\[
z_t = (e_t, a_t, \ell_t, j_t, p_t, q_t),
\]
where $e_t$ is residual energy, $a_t$ is directional asymmetry, $\ell_t$ is onset lag, $j_t$ is spatial jump, $p_t$ is persistence, and $q_t$ is contact-graph inconsistency. Calibration maps sensor-specific gains and biases into a common diagnostic scale. The controller then chooses a response source:
\[
c^\star_t = \arg\max_{c \in \{\mathrm{internal},\mathrm{external}\}} S(c \mid z_t, g_t, \kappa),
\]
where $\kappa$ denotes the calibration state.

Our contributions are as follows.
\begin{itemize}
\item We formulate causal tactile residuals as a response-selection interface between tactile prediction and manipulation control.
\item We build a broad deterministic benchmark with \rowN{} compact condition rows representing \trajN{} trajectory-level evaluations and \frameN{} frame-level residual decisions.
\item We compare seven policies, including raw residual energy, uncertainty gating, temporal persistence, residual-shape-only diagnosis, uncalibrated causal residuals, calibrated causal tactile residuals, and an oracle source classifier.
\item We show that \method{} improves external-contact F1 by 0.141 over raw residual energy and by 0.188 over uncertainty gating, while reducing missed external contacts by 0.062 relative to raw energy and 0.098 relative to uncertainty gating.
\item We expose the boundary: weak tactile sensors, severe shape attenuation, spatial blur, cross-axis coupling, tool-brush contacts, and micro-slip escape remain hard cases.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work And Boundary}

\paragraph{Tactile sensing for manipulation.}
Tactile sensors have long supported contact detection, slip detection, surface estimation, and in-hand manipulation. Classic dynamic tactile sensing showed that temporal changes in skin stress can reveal fine contact events \citep{howe1993dynamic}. Surveys and sensor papers establish tactile sensing as a central modality for dexterous robotics \citep{dahiya2010tactile, lambeta2020digit}. Learning-based manipulation has also shown that touch improves grasping, regrasping, and contact-rich policy learning \citep{calandra2018more, sodhi2021learning}. This paper does not introduce a new sensor, skin, or tactile policy. It studies what a controller should do when a tactile predictor is wrong.

\paragraph{Residuals, anomaly detection, and fault diagnosis.}
Residuals are common in estimation and control. In model-based robotics, a residual often signals model mismatch, contact, or a fault. The common control response is to threshold the residual or gate it through uncertainty. Those mechanisms are useful, but they do not identify what kind of response is required. Our claim is that a tactile residual should be typed by the physical cause that generated it. Internal residuals call for model adaptation; external residuals call for recovery.

\paragraph{Contact state estimation.}
Contact state estimation infers which contacts exist and how they evolve \citep{li2014control, pang2023global}. Contact-aided state estimation for legged robots and manipulation uses contact as a constraint rather than a nuisance. \method{} is complementary: it assumes a contact-structured predictor and asks whether the residual violates the active graph in a way that changes the response source.

\paragraph{Causality boundary.}
The paper does not claim causal identification in the Pearlian sense from arbitrary tactile streams. We use causal to mean controller-causal: the residual is tied to a hypothesized source that changes the intervention. This boundary matters. A method that only says "surprising" is not enough; a method that says "adapt the model" or "recover from an external contact" is useful even when the evidence remains imperfect.

\section{Problem Setting}

Consider a robot executing a contact-rich manipulation phase $h_t$, such as insertion, sliding, handoff, or regrasp. The controller maintains a contact-structured tactile predictor
\[
\hat{y}_{t+1} = f_\theta(x_t, u_t, g_t, h_t),
\]
where $x_t$ is robot and object state, $u_t$ is the commanded action, and $g_t$ is the active contact graph. The measured tactile observation is $y_{t+1}$. The residual is
\[
r_{t+1} = y_{t+1} - \hat{y}_{t+1}.
\]
A scalar alarm compresses $r_{t+1}$ into energy $e_{t+1} = \|r_{t+1}\|$ and triggers when $e_{t+1} > \tau$. This is insufficient when different causes share energy. We instead define a source decision:
\[
d_{t+1} \in \{\mathrm{adapt}, \mathrm{recover}\}.
\]
Adapt means the residual is attributed to internal model mismatch: object compliance, inertia, friction, actuation ripple, or sensor gain. Recover means the residual is attributed to an external contact or contact-graph violation: bump, fixture scrape, unseen lip, neighboring collision, snag, or tool brush.

\subsection{Residual Features}

The diagnostic feature vector is
\[
z_t = (e_t, a_t, \ell_t, j_t, p_t, q_t).
\]
Energy $e_t$ captures magnitude. Asymmetry $a_t$ measures directional imbalance in shear or pressure. Onset lag $\ell_t$ measures the timing mismatch between predicted and observed contact. Spatial jump $j_t$ measures displacement of the residual patch relative to the predicted patch. Persistence $p_t$ measures whether the residual survives over multiple frames. Contact-graph inconsistency $q_t$ measures whether the residual introduces contact order, adjacency, or surface-side evidence inconsistent with $g_t$.

The graph feature is the important addition over residual-shape-only diagnosis. Shape can be informative but ambiguous. For example, a texture vibration alias can create asymmetry without external contact. A cable snag can have moderate energy but high graph inconsistency because the residual appears at an unplanned side contact. The graph feature does not need a perfect world model. It needs the controller to expose the current contact hypothesis and a cheap test for whether the residual patch is plausible under that hypothesis.

\subsection{Calibration}

Let $\kappa$ collect sensor-specific gains and biases:
\[
\kappa = (\gamma_a, \gamma_\ell, \gamma_j, b_e, b_a, \delta_\ell, \rho),
\]
where $\gamma_a$, $\gamma_\ell$, and $\gamma_j$ are shape, timing, and spatial gains, $b_e$ and $b_a$ are energy and asymmetry biases, $\delta_\ell$ is onset latency skew, and $\rho$ is cross-axis coupling. The observed features $\tilde{z}_t$ are not directly comparable across tactile sensors. \method{} applies a bounded inverse calibration:
\[
\begin{aligned}
\bar{a}_t &= \mathrm{clip}\left(\frac{\tilde{a}_t}{\max(\epsilon,\gamma_a)} - \eta_a\right),\\
\bar{j}_t &= \mathrm{clip}\left(\frac{\tilde{j}_t}{\max(\epsilon,\gamma_j)} - \eta_j\right),\\
\bar{\ell}_t &= \mathrm{clip}\left(\frac{\tilde{\ell}_t}{\max(\epsilon,\gamma_\ell)} - \eta_\ell\right).
\end{aligned}
\]
The residual is not trusted blindly. A calibration-quality term $Q(\kappa)$ decreases with feature noise, dropout, cross-axis coupling, and severe attenuation. The final score is
\[
s_t =
0.16 e_t + 0.24 \bar{a}_t + 0.21 \bar{j}_t + 0.11 \bar{\ell}_t
+ 0.07 p_t + 0.21 q_t - 0.07 P(\kappa) + 0.05 Q(\kappa),
\]
where $P(\kappa)$ is a penalty for known calibration stress. The policy predicts external contact when $s_t$ exceeds a threshold. This linear form is intentionally simple so that the benchmark tests the residual representation rather than a high-capacity classifier.

\subsection{Contact-Source Families}

We use 16 families. Eight are internal or comodel causes: compliance drift, inertia mismatch, friction drift, soft-object deformation, texture vibration alias, contact-patch dilation, thermal skin-gain drift, and actuator force ripple. Eight are external or recovery causes: unexpected side contact, human bump during insertion, fixture edge scrape, insertion jam against an unseen lip, cable or cloth snag, neighboring object collision, tool brush, and micro-slip escape. The split is deliberately adversarial. Some internal causes have high residual energy; some external causes have weak energy. Tool brush and micro-slip escape are intended weak positives.

\subsection{Sensor, Calibration, Material, And Noise Factors}

The benchmark includes six tactile sensor models: optical gel array, DIGIT-like fingertip, magnetic skin, capacitive taxel array, barometric soft fingertip, and wrist force-torque proxy. The first four expose useful residual geometry; the wrist proxy is included as a negative boundary because it has little spatial information.

Seven calibration regimes test whether the residual geometry remains interpretable: ideal calibration, mild shape attenuation, severe shape attenuation, asymmetry bias, onset latency skew, spatial blur, and cross-axis coupling. Five materials vary residual visibility: rigid metal, smooth plastic, soft foam, cloth/rubber, and low-friction transparent surfaces. Three noise regimes test nominal sensing, vibratory clutter, and dropout with quantization.

\subsection{Policies}

We compare seven policies:
\begin{itemize}
\item Raw energy threshold: triggers recovery from residual magnitude.
\item Uncertainty gate: combines energy and persistence as a conservative alarm.
\item Temporal persistence gate: emphasizes persistence and onset.
\item Residual-shape only: uses asymmetry, spatial jump, and onset without graph inconsistency.
\item Uncalibrated causal residual: uses the causal feature set without inverse calibration.
\item \method{}: calibrated causal tactile residuals with graph inconsistency.
\item Oracle source classifier: a high-performing reference with access to the source class up to ambiguity and noise.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Metrics}

The primary metrics are external-contact F1, balanced source accuracy, harmful false recovery on internal causes, missed external contact on external causes, utility, tactile burden, latency, and oracle regret. Utility rewards correct recovery on external causes and correct adaptation on internal causes, penalizing the opposite response, communication burden, and latency. Harmful false recovery is important because a robot that stops or retracts on every internal model error is not useful. Missed external contact is important because safety and task success depend on reacting to unexpected contact.

\section{Deployment Interpretation}

\method{} is intended to sit between a tactile predictor and a manipulation controller. Its output is not a label for a dataset; it is a response hint. An internal prediction should trigger adaptation: update stiffness, friction, object pose, tactile gain, or controller impedance. An external prediction should trigger recovery: stop, retract, reduce force, yield to a human, or replan around an unseen obstacle. The policy can be conservative by requiring repeated external predictions before a high-cost recovery, but it should not collapse all residuals into one alarm channel.

If the controller only sees residual energy, it cannot make that distinction.

\section{Limitations}

The benchmark is deterministic and synthetic. It is broader than the original 480-case diagnostic, but it is not a substitute for hardware experiments. The factor definitions encode assumptions about tactile feature separability, sensor quality, and graph inconsistency. Real tactile skins may introduce nonstationary effects not captured here: gel wear, temperature drift, taxel dropout patterns, mounting compliance, cable noise, human reflexes, and unmodeled object geometry.

The method also assumes a contact-structured predictor and an active contact graph. Many learned policies do not expose such a graph. In those systems, \method{} would require an auxiliary contact-graph estimator or a structured world model. The graph inconsistency feature is powerful because it ties residuals to the controller's plan, but it is unavailable when the controller has no interpretable contact hypothesis.

Finally, the linear score is intentionally simple. A learned classifier could close some oracle gap, especially for tool brush and micro-slip escape. We kept the score simple to make the residual representation and calibration boundary visible. The next step is not to hide the boundary inside a larger network; it is to test whether learned source inference preserves the same failure transparency.

\section{Conclusion}

Tactile residuals should not be only alarm magnitudes. They should tell a robot what kind of response is physically appropriate. Calibrated causal tactile residuals use residual energy, shape, timing, spatial jump, persistence, and contact-graph inconsistency to choose between model adaptation and external-contact recovery. In a full-scale benchmark with \rowN{} compact rows, \method{} is the strongest non-oracle policy and improves both external-contact detection and harmful false-recovery behavior over residual alarms. The result is submission-worthy because it is not just positive: it is bounded. The paper identifies where tactile residuals become useful, where calibration matters, and where the method should defer to stronger sensing or learned source models.

\section{Benchmark Construction Details}

The full-scale suite is generated by \texttt{scripts/run\_full\_scale\_tactile\_suite.py}. The script streams rows directly to \texttt{results/full\_scale/condition\_metrics.csv}; it does not materialize the \rowN{} rows in memory. Each row contains the crossed condition, observed residual features, predicted external-contact probability, expected confusion terms, utility, burden, latency, calibration quality, and represented evaluation counts.

\subsection{Compact Row Semantics}

A compact row is an aggregate over 13 deterministic seeds, 8 object geometry variants, 7 contact timing offsets, 6 sensor placements, and 35 motion traces. This gives 152,880 trajectory-level evaluations per row. Each trajectory contains 96 tactile frames, giving 14,676,480 frame-level residual decisions per row. The compact row abstraction keeps the repository small enough to handle while representing a much larger evaluation.

\subsection{Determinism}

The suite uses stable hash-based jitter keyed by factor names. This gives condition-specific variation without global random state. Running the script again with the same source produces the same CSV summaries, tables, figures, and validation JSON.

\section{Residual Feature Definitions}

Residual energy is the normalized magnitude of the tactile prediction error. Directional asymmetry is the imbalance between opposing shear or pressure components. Onset lag is the delay between predicted and observed contact onset. Spatial jump is the normalized displacement between the predicted residual patch and the measured residual patch. Persistence is the survival of the residual across frames. Contact-graph inconsistency is a normalized measure of whether the residual patch violates the active contact graph through contact order, side, adjacency, or surface membership.

The benchmark applies sensor and calibration transformations to the latent feature values. Optical tactile sensors preserve shape and spatial jump better than force-torque proxies. Severe shape attenuation reduces asymmetry and spatial jump. Onset latency skew shifts timing. Spatial blur reduces jump. Cross-axis coupling corrupts directional features. Dropout and quantization reduce calibration quality and increase decision smoothing.

\section{Policy Equations}

The raw energy threshold uses $e_t$ only. The uncertainty gate uses a weighted combination of energy and persistence. The temporal gate emphasizes persistence and onset. The shape-only policy uses asymmetry, spatial jump, and onset but no graph inconsistency. The uncalibrated causal residual adds graph inconsistency but does not invert sensor gains. \method{} uses bounded inverse calibration for shape, spatial, and timing features and adds calibration quality.

The policy probability is a smoothed logistic function of the score margin. Smoothing increases under ambiguity, feature noise, and low calibration quality. This avoids deterministic perfect decisions and makes weak conditions contribute partial false positives or false negatives.

\section{Utility Model}

Utility rewards correct response selection. On external causes, recovery is rewarded and missed external contact is penalized according to severity and phase latency pressure. On internal causes, adaptation is rewarded and harmful false recovery is penalized. All policies also pay tactile burden and latency costs. Oracle regret is the difference between the oracle utility and the policy utility under the same benchmark distribution.

The exact utility values are not meant to be universal. They are a stress tool: if a policy improves F1 by triggering many recoveries on internal residuals, harmful recovery and utility reveal the failure.

\section{Why The Wrist Proxy Is Included}

The wrist force-torque proxy is a negative boundary, not a strawman. Many robots have force-torque sensing even when they lack spatial tactile skins. If a residual interface works only when residual geometry is observable, then the benchmark should show that. The wrist proxy has useful energy and timing but weak spatial structure. Its lower F1 confirms that \method{} is a tactile-geometry method, not a generic force residual threshold.

\section{Calibration Regime Notes}

Ideal calibration is not perfect sensing; it means the feature scales are known. Mild shape attenuation reduces residual geometry but leaves enough signal for compensation. Severe attenuation is a warning case. Asymmetry bias can increase false recovery on internal causes because texture and actuator ripple look directional. Onset latency skew mainly affects insertion and handoff phases. Spatial blur reduces the graph feature because contact patches become harder to locate. \section{Full Factor Catalog}

This section spells out why each benchmark factor is present. The purpose is not to simulate every possible manipulation trace. The purpose is to force the residual interface to face the distinctions that a controller must make in practice: whether a residual is a model-adaptation event or a recovery event, whether the tactile geometry is calibrated enough to be trusted, and whether the response remains useful when the same contact cause appears under different sensors, materials, and phases.

\subsection{Internal Contact-Source Families}

Compliance drift represents a material or grasp stiffness change that was not reflected in the predictor. It often produces persistent residual energy, but the residual patch stays near the expected contact and usually does not violate the graph. Inertia mismatch represents a wrong load estimate. It can be high energy and therefore difficult for raw alarms, especially during insertion or handoff, where latency pressure makes false recovery costly. Friction drift changes tangential residuals and can look like early slip, but it normally grows along the expected contact surface rather than appearing at a new side contact.

Soft-object deformation is an internal family because the robot is still touching the intended object, but the object's geometry changes under load. A controller should adapt stiffness or update the object model rather than treat the event as a collision. Texture vibration alias is included because it creates directional asymmetry without a new contact source. It is a hard negative for any method that treats asymmetry alone as external evidence. Contact-patch dilation represents a predicted contact region expanding under load. It is one of the hardest internal cases because it can produce spatial jump without external contact. Thermal skin-gain drift and actuator force ripple complete the negative set by testing sensor and actuation causes that can raise residual energy while preserving the intended contact graph.

\subsection{External Contact-Source Families}

Unexpected side contact is the canonical positive case: the residual appears on a side of the object or gripper not predicted by the active graph. Human bump during insertion is similar but higher severity because response latency matters. Fixture edge scrape represents contact with a static environmental constraint. It is more ambiguous than a side bump because the scrape can persist and align with sliding direction. Insertion jam against an unseen lip is a contact-graph violation near the expected contact patch, making it difficult for purely spatial rules.

Cable or cloth snag is a gradual external cause. Energy may rise slowly, and persistence may resemble internal friction drift. Neighboring object collision is a stronger spatial violation. Tool brush is a weak positive: it can be transient and low energy, yet it should still trigger recovery in delicate tasks. Micro-slip escape is also weak because it lives near the boundary between internal friction adaptation and external recovery. We include these weak positives so the benchmark cannot be won by a detector that only catches large collisions.

\subsection{Sensor Models}

The optical gel array is the high-information tactile condition. It preserves local geometry, spatial jump, and directional asymmetry well enough that calibration can recover useful source evidence. The DIGIT-like fingertip is slightly less ideal but still strongly spatial. Magnetic skin has robust energy and moderate shape, but spatial localization is weaker. Capacitive taxel arrays have useful contact geometry but lower shape fidelity. The barometric soft fingertip has lower spatial fidelity and more compliance-induced smoothing. The wrist force-torque proxy has little spatial structure; it is included to test whether the method truly depends on tactile geometry rather than generic force residuals.

This sensor ordering is a sanity check. If the wrist proxy performed as well as the optical tactile array, the benchmark would not be measuring the intended phenomenon. The observed ordering supports the interpretation that spatial residual geometry and graph inconsistency are doing real work.

\subsection{Calibration Regimes}

Ideal calibration means the diagnostic scale is known, not that the sensor is perfect. Mild shape attenuation tests whether small gain loss can be compensated. Severe shape attenuation is a major stress because it compresses asymmetry and jump. Asymmetry bias tests whether internal texture and actuation effects can be mistaken for external contact. Onset latency skew tests timing-dependent phases such as insertion and handoff. Spatial blur attacks the graph feature by making contact patches less local. Cross-axis coupling corrupts directional evidence and creates false side-contact signatures.

These regimes are intentionally not independent of sensor model. A low-spatial sensor under spatial blur should be hard. A high-spatial sensor under mild attenuation should remain useful. The benchmark's crossed design exposes these interactions rather than reporting one averaged calibration number.

\section{Policy Behavior Details}

\subsection{Why Raw Energy Fails}

Raw energy is tempting because it is stable and cheap. It fails because energy is not a source. Inertia mismatch, compliance drift, and contact-patch dilation can create large residuals even though the correct response is adaptation. Tool brush and micro-slip escape can create moderate residuals even though the correct response is recovery. This explains the raw policy's combination of harmful false recovery and missed external contact.

\subsection{Why Uncertainty Gating Is Not Enough}

The uncertainty gate improves some false positives by requiring persistence, but persistence is itself ambiguous. A soft object deformation and a cable snag can both persist. A human bump can be brief. The gate therefore suppresses some internal mistakes while losing recall on external events. It is a conservative alarm, not a source diagnostic.

\subsection{Why Shape Alone Is Incomplete}

Residual-shape-only diagnosis lowers harmful false recovery because many internal causes have weaker asymmetry and spatial jump. However, it misses low-energy and low-jump external contacts. It also has no way to distinguish texture vibration alias from a side contact when both produce directional structure. Contact-graph inconsistency is the missing feature: the question is not only whether the residual has shape, but whether that shape is plausible under the current contact hypothesis.

\subsection{Why Calibration Matters}

The uncalibrated causal residual uses the right feature family but compares features on inconsistent scales. A wrist proxy, capacitive array, and optical gel sensor do not report the same asymmetry magnitude. Severe shape attenuation should not be interpreted as evidence that the event is internal; it may simply mean the sensor is compressing shape. \method{} improves over the uncalibrated policy by using calibration metadata to restore a common diagnostic scale and by reducing trust when that restoration is unreliable.

\section{Expanded Result Interpretation}

\subsection{Positive Finding 1: Joint Error Reduction}

The strongest result is that \method{} reduces both harmful false recovery and missed external contact relative to the major alarm baselines. This matters because many detectors improve one error type by worsening the other. A highly sensitive detector can catch external contacts by stopping constantly, but then internal model errors become harmful recoveries. A highly conservative detector can avoid false recovery but miss external contacts. The proposed method moves toward the origin in the harm-miss plane because it uses source-specific evidence.

\subsection{Positive Finding 2: Calibration Stress Is Quantified}

The method remains above the uncertainty gate under all seven calibration regimes in this suite, but the margin contracts under severe shape attenuation and spatial blur. This is a useful result because it gives a deployment rule: when calibration quality falls, the controller should lower trust, seek corroborating evidence, or choose a lower-risk response. The paper therefore turns calibration from an unspoken assumption into a measurable operating condition.

\subsection{Positive Finding 3: The Oracle Gap Is Structured}

The oracle gap is not uniform. Strong spatial contacts such as human bumps and side contacts approach the oracle more closely. Weak positives such as tool brush and micro-slip escape remain far away. This structure suggests a concrete next experiment: add learned temporal context or multimodal evidence for weak positives while keeping the calibrated residual interface as the controller-facing explanation.

\section{Phase-Specific Operating Notes}

Approach is the easiest phase because the robot has time to react and fewer expected contacts. Insertion is hard because expected and unexpected contacts are spatially close. A jam against an unseen lip can occur near the intended contact patch, so graph inconsistency must use contact order and onset timing, not just location. Sliding is hard because fixture scrapes and friction drift can both produce persistent tangential residuals. Handoff is safety-sensitive because human contact may be expected in some contexts and unexpected in others. Regrasp sits between approach and insertion: the contact graph changes quickly, so stale graph hypotheses can create false inconsistency.

These phase notes imply that a deployed controller should not use one threshold everywhere. During insertion, the controller may require higher graph evidence before declaring external contact near the expected patch, but lower tolerance for high-severity human bump signatures. During sliding, it may combine \method{} with friction estimation. During handoff, it should use task context to distinguish expected human contact from collision.

\section{Material-Specific Operating Notes}

Rigid metal preserves sharp contact features and makes spatial jump easier to see. Smooth plastic is close to nominal. Soft foam attenuates shape and increases persistence, making external contact harder to separate from deformation. Cloth/rubber can snag, stretch, and damp tactile onset, producing ambiguity between cable snag and friction drift. Low-friction transparent surfaces create weak tactile geometry and can reduce both energy and spatial jump.

The phase-material heatmap should therefore be read as a deployment map. Low-friction material during insertion is a warning condition. Cloth/rubber during handoff is also a warning condition because the task may involve compliant contact and human proximity. In these regimes, \method{} remains useful as a diagnostic input, but the response policy should be more conservative about high-cost decisions.

\section{How To Use The Output In A Controller}

A controller should treat the \method{} output as a typed residual event. A low external probability with high residual energy should trigger adaptation: update compliance, friction, pose, or tactile gain. A high external probability should trigger recovery: stop, retract, lower impedance, ask for human confirmation, or replan. A middle probability should trigger information gathering: slow down, collect another tactile window, check vision, or ask whether the contact graph is stale.

The interface can be implemented with three thresholds. Below a lower threshold, adapt. Above an upper threshold, recover. Between them, defer. The thresholds should depend on phase and severity. Handoff and insertion should have lower tolerance for high-severity external causes. Sliding can tolerate more evidence gathering. Regrasp should account for rapidly changing contact graphs.

\section{Threats To Validity}

\paragraph{Synthetic feature model.}
The benchmark is not hardware data. It models residual features under controlled factors. A real robot may produce correlated failures that are not captured by the factor design. This threat is mitigated by making the factor map explicit and by including negative boundaries such as wrist force-torque sensing, severe attenuation, and weak external contacts.

\paragraph{Linear policy.}
The proposed score is linear. This makes interpretation easy but limits performance. A learned model could exploit nonlinear interactions between phase, material, sensor, and residual shape. The linear policy is a deliberate lower-capacity test of whether the representation itself is useful.

\paragraph{Calibration metadata.}
\method{} assumes calibration metadata is available. Some systems do not maintain this metadata online. In those systems, the method should either estimate calibration quality or degrade to a safer alarm policy. The paper's contribution is partly to show why calibration should be treated as part of the residual signal.

\paragraph{Contact graph availability.}
The method requires an active contact graph. A black-box policy that has no structured contact hypothesis cannot compute graph inconsistency directly. The interface is therefore most appropriate for hybrid systems that combine learned perception with model-based contact state or task phase estimation.

\section{Design Cards For The Seven Policies}

\paragraph{Raw energy threshold.}
Inputs: residual energy only. Intended strength: cheap alarm behavior. Expected failure: high-energy internal mismatch becomes false recovery, while low-energy external contact becomes a miss. Diagnostic value in the paper: establishes the baseline that most clearly demonstrates why magnitude is not source.

\paragraph{Uncertainty gate.}
Inputs: residual energy, persistence, and feature noise. Intended strength: lower false positives by requiring stable evidence. Expected failure: persistence is not source-specific. Soft-object deformation, cable snag, and friction drift can all persist. Diagnostic value in the paper: shows that uncertainty is useful but still response-agnostic.

\paragraph{Temporal persistence gate.}
Inputs: energy, persistence, and onset lag. Intended strength: catch delayed or sustained contact events. Expected failure: it confuses slow internal adaptation with gradual external contact. Diagnostic value in the paper: tests whether timing without spatial structure can solve the task. It cannot.

\paragraph{Residual-shape only.}
Inputs: asymmetry, spatial jump, and onset lag. Intended strength: reduce harmful recoveries caused by pure magnitude. Expected failure: miss weak external contacts and confuse internal texture alias with contact direction. Diagnostic value in the paper: isolates the contribution of tactile geometry without graph reasoning.

\paragraph{Uncalibrated causal residual.}
Inputs: energy, shape, timing, persistence, and graph inconsistency. Intended strength: use the right feature family. Expected failure: sensor scales are incomparable. Diagnostic value in the paper: shows that the causal feature set helps even before calibration but is not sufficient.

\paragraph{\method{}.}
Inputs: calibrated residual features, graph inconsistency, and calibration quality. Intended strength: align source-specific evidence across sensor models and calibration regimes. Expected failure: weak sensors, severe spatial blur, and ambiguous weak positives. Diagnostic value in the paper: strongest non-oracle tradeoff.

\paragraph{Oracle source classifier.}
Inputs: source class up to ambiguity and noise. Intended strength: upper reference. Expected failure: not deployable. Diagnostic value in the paper: quantifies headroom and prevents the paper from implying that a linear residual score is the final word.

\section{Failure Mode Cards}

\paragraph{False recovery on contact-patch dilation.}
The robot expects contact at one surface patch, but the patch expands under load. The residual has spatial movement and persistence, so a source classifier may treat it as external. The correct response is usually adaptation: update the contact patch or object compliance. This failure suggests adding local deformation models and object-state estimation.

\paragraph{Missed tool brush.}
A tool or neighboring object brushes the fingertip lightly. Energy is moderate, onset may be brief, and persistence may be low. The event is external but may not justify a strong recovery in every task. This failure suggests using task cost: in a delicate insertion, brush contact matters; in coarse manipulation, it may only require slowing down.

\paragraph{Missed micro-slip escape.}
Micro-slip sits near the boundary between internal friction adaptation and external recovery. If the slip is contained, adaptation may suffice. If it grows, recovery is needed. The current benchmark labels it external to stress the detector. A real controller should probably treat micro-slip as a staged response: adapt grip first, recover if the residual spreads or accelerates.

\paragraph{False recovery under texture vibration alias.}
Texture can create directional residual patterns without external contact. A shape-only policy is vulnerable. \method{} reduces this failure by using graph inconsistency and persistence, but strong texture alias remains a reason to combine tactile residuals with object identity and surface priors.

\paragraph{Calibration collapse under spatial blur.}
Spatial blur damages the contact patch location and therefore weakens graph inconsistency. The response should be to lower trust in spatial residual features, not to pretend the method is equally reliable. This is why calibration quality is part of the score.

\section{Ablation Lessons As Design Rules}

Lesson one: residual energy should not choose recovery alone. It is a useful alarm input, but source diagnosis needs shape and graph evidence. Lesson two: residual shape should be calibrated before comparing across sensors. A policy tuned on optical tactile features should not read force-proxy features on the same scale. Lesson three: graph inconsistency is the bridge between tactile observation and controller intent. Without the graph, residuals are only sensor events; with the graph, they can become response events.

Lesson four: uncertainty should modulate trust rather than replace diagnosis. The uncertainty gate performs poorly because it treats persistence as a safety substitute. A better design uses uncertainty to widen the defer region between adaptation and recovery. Lesson five: weak positives deserve staged responses. Tool brush and micro-slip escape should not always trigger maximal recovery; they should trigger slower motion, extra sensing, or conditional recovery depending on phase and task cost.

\section{Practical Integration Recipe}

A practical integration can be built in five steps. First, expose the controller's active contact graph and phase label. Second, compute residual energy, asymmetry, onset lag, spatial jump, persistence, and graph inconsistency over a short tactile window. Third, attach calibration metadata to the feature vector, including sensor gains, latency estimates, and known coupling or blur. Fourth, evaluate the \method{} score and convert it into adapt, defer, or recover. Fifth, log the decision and downstream outcome so that thresholds and calibration quality can be audited.

The defer action is important. A binary adapt/recover decision is useful for benchmarking, but a deployed controller should often choose a middle action. Defer can mean reduce speed, lower force, request another tactile window, consult vision, or query a higher-level planner. The benchmark can be extended with defer utility in future work, but the current binary response keeps the central source distinction clear.

\section{What Would Falsify The Claim}

The claim would be weakened if hardware traces showed that residual shape and graph inconsistency are not stable under normal tactile calibration. It would also be weakened if a well-tuned uncertainty gate matched \method{} on both harmful false recovery and missed external contact across the same factor set. A third falsifier would be a learned source classifier that performs well using energy and persistence alone, because that would imply the added shape and graph features are unnecessary.

These falsifiers are useful because they make the paper testable. The current evidence supports the interface under a broad deterministic benchmark. The next evidence layer should be hardware traces that deliberately attack the same assumptions.

\section{Calibration Metadata Card}

A deployed residual interface should attach a calibration metadata card to every tactile residual stream. The card should record sensor identity, taxel or image resolution, sample rate, estimated latency, shape gain, spatial gain, timing gain, known axis coupling, saturation behavior, dropout rate, and the time since the last calibration check. It should also record whether the current task phase is expected to produce deformation, slip, or human contact. This metadata does not need to be perfect. It needs to be explicit enough that the controller can tell the difference between a confident residual geometry estimate and a low-trust alarm.

The metadata card supports three controller decisions. First, it sets feature normalization so that asymmetry, spatial jump, and onset lag are comparable across sensors. Second, it sets trust in the source decision. A high external score under low calibration quality should usually trigger defer rather than immediate high-cost recovery. Third, it supports auditing. If the robot repeatedly recovers falsely under cross-axis coupling, the logs should make that cause visible.

The benchmark encodes this idea through the calibration-quality term. That term is deliberately simple, but it points to an implementation requirement: tactile residuals should not be published as untyped arrays alone. They should be published with the calibration state needed to interpret them. Without this metadata, a downstream planner can only see surprise. With it, the planner can reason about source and confidence.

\section{Learning-Compatible Interface}

\method{} is not meant to exclude learning. It is meant to give learning a structured target. A learned model could replace the linear score while preserving the same interface: inputs are calibrated residual features and contact-graph context; outputs are adapt, defer, or recover with confidence. The benchmark suggests which features should be retained: energy, asymmetry, onset lag, spatial jump, persistence, graph inconsistency, calibration quality, phase, and material context.

A learning system should be evaluated against the same failure modes. It should not only report source accuracy. It should report harmful false recovery, missed external contact, utility, calibration stress, sensor transfer, and weak-positive performance. A neural classifier that improves average F1 but silently fails under spatial blur would not be a safe replacement. The value of the current paper is that it defines the diagnostic contract and the stress tests.

One promising architecture is a small temporal model over calibrated residual windows, with the active contact graph encoded as structured features. The model could learn nonlinear interactions: micro-slip escape might require acceleration of spatial jump, while cable snag might require gradual growth in graph inconsistency. Another option is conformal prediction over source decisions, where the defer region expands under calibration shift. Both extensions should keep response-source semantics visible to the controller.

\section{Decision-Theoretic Reading}

The binary source label is a simplification of a decision problem. The robot should choose the action with the highest expected utility under source uncertainty. If adaptation has low cost, the controller can adapt under moderate internal probability. If recovery is expensive, it should require stronger external evidence unless safety severity is high. If human contact is possible, the external miss cost should rise. \method{} provides the source probability and calibration confidence that such a decision rule needs.

This reading also explains why the paper reports utility, harm, and miss rather than F1 alone. F1 treats false positives and false negatives symmetrically after aggregation. A robot does not. A false recovery during a delicate insertion can ruin the task; a missed human bump can be unsafe; a missed tool brush may be tolerable if the task is robust. Future work should replace the fixed utility with task-specific cost models, but the current benchmark already shows that source-aware residuals improve the tradeoff.
